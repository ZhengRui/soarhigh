# Toastmasters Evaluation

Evaluation is the **educational core** of a Toastmasters meeting. Speakers grow because they get specific, goal-aligned, future-facing feedback — not because they spoke. A meeting can have brilliant speeches and still be a weak meeting if evaluations are vague, harsh, or off-target. Conversely, a meeting with average speeches and razor-sharp evaluations is a great meeting.

Three roles do evaluation:

- **Individual Evaluator (IE)** — evaluates ONE specific Prepared Speaker.
- **Table Topics Evaluator (TTE)** — evaluates the entire Table Topics segment, speaker by speaker.
- **General Evaluator (GE)** — evaluates the meeting itself, with focused feedback on three role-groups + overall flow.

This skill covers methodology shared across all three, then per-role specifics, then pitfalls and prep.

## What evaluation IS (and isn't)

| Evaluation IS | Evaluation is NOT |
|---|---|
| Specific — "you used a story about your grandmother that landed in 30 seconds and pulled the audience in" | Vague — "good speech" / "well done" |
| Goal-aligned — anchored to the speaker's stated objectives | Personal-preference — "I would have done it this way" |
| Balanced — both concrete commendations AND concrete recommendations | One-sided — only praise, or only criticism |
| Future-facing — "next time, try X" | Retroactive — "you should have done X" |
| Brief — 2–3 min IE, 5–8 min TTE / GE | Over-run — a 4-minute IE blows the agenda |
| Audience-first — addresses the room, then references the speaker | Speaker-first — "X, you were great" without framing |

**Evaluators do NOT score, grade, or rank.** No "7/10", no "best speech of the night" calls (that's the audience's voting later), no league-tabling speakers against each other. Targeted feedback only.

## The sandwich structure

Standard format for every evaluation, regardless of role:

```
Address audience → Commendation 1 → Recommendation → Commendation 2 → Close
```

- **Address the audience first**, not the speaker. Toastmasters conventionally opens: "Mr./Madam Toastmaster, fellow Toastmasters and dear guests, today's evaluation is for [speaker name]…" The speaker is referenced in the third person at first; you're talking ABOUT them to the room.
- **Commendation 1** — one specific thing that worked. Specific = observable + recent + tied to a moment in the speech.
- **Recommendation** — the meat. One concrete, actionable change. NOT "your delivery could be better" — that's vague. Instead: "next time, try pausing for 2 seconds before your punchline — it gives the audience time to anticipate the laugh."
- **Commendation 2** — a second specific positive, ideally different from the first (content vs delivery; structure vs storytelling). Closes on a high note.
- **Close** — brief: "Overall, [speaker] delivered a [adjective] speech. Madam Toastmaster, back to you."

The recommendation is the load-bearing piece — that's what helps the speaker grow. The commendations frame it so the recommendation lands as helpful, not punitive.

**Why this structure works:** without the bookend commendations, even a fair recommendation can read as harsh. With them, the speaker hears the recommendation as one of three observations rather than as a verdict.

## Pathways-aware evaluation

Toastmasters speeches since 2018 come from **Pathways projects** with stated learning objectives. Critical for IEs:

- Each speech is assigned to a path (DL / EH / MS / PI / PM / VC) and a project (e.g. `DL L1P2 — Evaluation and Feedback`).
- Each project has 2–4 learning objectives the speaker is practicing.
- **The IE evaluates AGAINST those objectives**, not against generic speech criteria.

Example: if the project is "Writing a Speech with Purpose" (Level 1, focused on structure), the IE focuses on structure — was there a clear opening, body, close? — and DOESN'T spend time critiquing humor or delivery (those are other projects' objectives). If the project is "Effective Body Language", the IE focuses on body language and minimizes feedback on speech content.

Without the objectives, the IE falls back to "how was that speech overall" — losing the entire Pathways advantage of targeted skill development.

**The Meeting Manager collects this info from each speaker before the meeting** (see `soarhigh-meeting-manager` for the prepared-speech info template). The IE should receive: path + level + project number + project objectives + the speaker's draft + any specific focus areas the speaker wants critiqued.

## Speaker-requested focus areas (SoarHigh convention)

A SoarHigh-supported practice from the role guide: **the speaker can tell their IE in advance what they specifically want feedback on**. E.g. "I'm worried my pacing is uneven — please watch for that." Or: "I'm not sure if my conclusion lands — let me know."

When the speaker requests a focus area, the IE should:

1. Confirm with the speaker that this remains the priority on meeting day (focus can shift after rehearsal).
2. Center the recommendation around that focus area, even if other things in the speech feel more "evaluable."
3. Briefly note that the focus area was speaker-requested — this signals to the audience that the IE is being respectful, not narrow.

This matches the Pathways philosophy: evaluation serves the speaker's learning goals, not the evaluator's preferences.

## Per-role specifics

### Individual Evaluator (IE)

- Evaluates **one** Prepared Speaker.
- **Bell band:** 2:00 / 2:30 / 3:00 (green / yellow / red), bell at red + 30s. **In practice IEs almost always run to the 3-minute red** — that's the expected length.
- Address audience first, then deliver the sandwich.
- Anchor to the project objectives (see Pathways-aware section above).
- Prep before the meeting: collect speech draft + project objectives + speaker's focus areas. Many IEs read the draft once for content, then watch the live speech for delivery — they evaluate the gap between intended and delivered.

### Table Topics Evaluator (TTE)

- Evaluates the **entire Table Topics segment, speaker by speaker** — the TTM's Opening AND each TT speaker individually. NOT a single whole-segment summary.
- **Total time 5–8 minutes** for the whole TTE block, distributed across all the speakers (so each individual evaluation is shorter than a full IE — typically 30–60 seconds per TT speaker).
- Bell band (whole block): 6:00 / 7:00 / 8:00, bell at 8:30 (3–10 min rule: green at 2 min left, yellow at 1 min left).
- Cannot prep against project objectives (Table Topics are impromptu) — instead, evaluate on:
  - Did the speaker engage with the prompt, or dodge it?
  - Was the structure (opening / point / close) clear despite no prep?
  - Was delivery confident under pressure?
  - Did they use stories / examples to make the impromptu vivid?
- The TTE often **takes notes during TT** so they can recall each speaker by name + key moment when evaluating.

### General Evaluator (GE)

- Evaluates the **meeting itself**, focused on three role-groups + overall flow:
  - **Facilitators** — Timer, AhCounter, Grammarian (and similar support roles). Did they introduce themselves clearly? Were their reports useful?
  - **Hosts** — Guest Introduction Host, Toastmaster of the Meeting (TOM), Table Topics Master (TTM). Did they bridge segments smoothly? Did the TOM keep energy up?
  - **Evaluators** — TTE and the Individual Evaluators (IEs). Was their feedback specific, balanced, future-facing? Did they address the audience first?
- Plus overall: agenda flow, transitions, time management, atmosphere.
- **Total time 5–8 minutes**. Bell band: 6:00 / 7:00 / 8:00, bell at 8:30 (3–10 min rule: green at 2 min left, yellow at 1 min left).
- The GE is **often the most experienced evaluator in the room** — they're evaluating the evaluators, which requires range.
- GE doesn't redo each IE/TTE evaluation — they assess WHETHER those evaluations were good (specific? balanced? on-time? respectful of speaker goals?).

## Common pitfalls

What evaluators trip over most often:

1. **Vague commendations.** "Good energy", "nice delivery", "great speech" — these tell the speaker nothing they can act on. Replace with one specific moment: "Your pause after 'and that's when I knew' gave the audience three seconds to anticipate — perfectly placed."

2. **Personal-preference recommendations.** "I would have used a different opening" is the evaluator's taste, not feedback. Instead: ground the recommendation in the speaker's project objectives or in audience reaction observable in the room.

3. **Evaluating delivery when the project is about structure** (or vice versa). Read the project objectives FIRST. If the project is "Writing a Speech with Purpose", spending 90 seconds on hand gestures wastes the speaker's learning opportunity.

4. **Speaker-first opening.** "Joyce, you did great tonight" — the audience is not the addressee. Open by addressing the room: "Mr. Toastmaster, fellow members and guests, today's evaluation is for Joyce…"

5. **Going over time.** A 4-minute IE pushes the meeting late and shows disrespect for the agenda. If the Timer's red is on, finish the current sentence and close. Discipline matters.

6. **Sandwich without a real recommendation.** "You did X well, you did Y well, and overall it was great" is two commendations, no growth feedback. The speaker leaves without anything to improve. Always include one concrete recommendation.

7. **Comparing speakers.** "Joyce was better at Y than Frank was last week" — never. Each evaluation is about ONE speaker on ONE speech.

8. **Score / rank language.** "I'd give this a B+", "this was the best speech tonight" — both wrong. The audience votes; evaluators describe and recommend.

9. **TTE doing whole-segment summary.** "The Table Topics tonight had high energy" is not a TTE evaluation — it's a vibe check. The TTE evaluates each TT speaker individually (with the TTM's Opening included).

10. **GE redoing the IE/TTE work.** "I also want to add about Joyce's speech…" — no. The GE evaluates HOW the IE evaluated Joyce, not Joyce's speech directly.

## Pre-meeting prep checklist

For evaluators preparing during the week before the meeting:

**IE (Individual Evaluator):**
- [ ] Confirm with the Meeting Manager which Prepared Speaker you're evaluating.
- [ ] Get the speaker's: path / level / project number, project objectives, speech title, draft, requested focus areas.
- [ ] Read the project objectives in the Pathways portal (or have the speaker summarize them).
- [ ] Read the draft once, NOT memorize it — you'll evaluate the live delivery against the intent the draft reveals.
- [ ] Pre-think: which of the project objectives is the speaker most likely to nail vs struggle with? Have a tentative recommendation framework — adjust on meeting day based on what actually happens.

**TTE (Table Topics Evaluator):**
- [ ] No advance content prep (TT is impromptu) — but bring a notepad.
- [ ] During TT: note each speaker's name + 1–2 key observations (a strong line, a stumble, a creative angle on the prompt).
- [ ] Plan your distribution: 5–8 minutes ÷ N speakers ≈ time per speaker. Prioritize specific feedback over coverage; if you have 8 speakers and only 5 min, focus on the 3–4 with the most teachable moments and acknowledge the rest briefly.

**GE (General Evaluator):**
- [ ] Receive the full agenda from the Meeting Manager.
- [ ] Identify which roles you'll observe most carefully (typically: facilitators get a quick scan, hosts + evaluators get more depth).
- [ ] During the meeting: take notes on transitions, time discipline, evaluator quality.
- [ ] Plan your three-group structure: 1 specific observation per group + 1 overall meeting-flow comment.

## Workshop / Custom meeting variants

- **Workshop meetings:** typically NO IE (no prepared speeches). Sometimes a custom evaluator gives feedback on the workshop itself — usually shorter and more conversational than a standard IE. TTE and GE may or may not run, depending on whether TT happened and whether time allows.
- **Custom meetings (debates, contests, themed nights):** evaluation roles depend on format. A debate night may have judges instead of evaluators. A contest may have official scoring rubrics (which DO use grades, unlike regular meetings — see contests below).

## Evaluation contests

Toastmasters runs **evaluation contests** at the area / division / district levels, where evaluators are themselves evaluated competitively. Contest rules differ from regular meetings:

- All evaluators evaluate the SAME test speech (a planted speaker prepared by the contest organizers).
- Evaluators are sequestered (don't hear each other's evaluations until after they deliver their own).
- **Strict timing**: in contests, the Timer signals silently (no bell), but going past the red+30s threshold **disqualifies** the evaluator — the bell is enforced as a hard rule.
- Judges score each evaluation against a rubric (specificity, structure, delivery, timeliness, recommendations).
- Higher-level contests progress winners up to the District championship.

For regular club meetings, none of this applies — evaluations there are educational, not competitive.

## What makes a "good" evaluator

Long-term, evaluators who repeatedly get nominated for Best Evaluator share these traits:

- **They listen first, judge second.** They observe the actual speech that happened, not the speech they pre-decided to evaluate.
- **They tie feedback to objectives.** Every recommendation traces to a Pathways objective or to a goal the speaker stated.
- **They're specific.** "Your pause at 1:42 was perfectly timed" beats "good pacing."
- **They respect the time-box.** Their evaluations land at red, not 30s past.
- **They're warm.** Sandwich isn't just structural — it's tonal. Speakers should feel evaluated, not attacked.
- **They follow up.** Out of meeting, they may message the speaker with one extra observation that didn't fit in 3 minutes — that's where deep mentorship happens.
